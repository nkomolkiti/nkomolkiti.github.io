ABOUT VERBAL ABUSE

Psychological abuse of a child is a pattern of intentional verbal or behavioral actions or lack of actions that convey to a child the message that he or she is worthless, flawed, unloved, unwanted, endangered, or only of value to meet someone else's needs. — Samantha Gluck, a journalist specializing in healthcare trends, mental health, health and fitness

Verbal abuse doesn't have to be loud; it can be quiet. Verbal abuse can be very harmful on children who haven't developed a conscious defense mechanism. The effects on the child can include, but not limited to, anxiety, depression, and self-criticism. Self-criticism is a mental habit of attributing all mistakes to yourself, such as "I failed because I am stupid" or "Nothing good will ever happen to me because I'm not good enough".

Sometimes it can come out as a joke (which is obviously not funny), or as a reflection of one's insecurity. The child might also view it as 'normal' and it becomes the continuation of action.

WHAT'S THE IMPACT?

The impact of verbal aggression and abuse tends to be discounted and marginalized; there seems to be an unspoken agreement that such abuse is "only words," as people cite the children's rhyme: "Sticks and stones may break my bones, but words shall never hurt me." This is not that people are not aware of verbal abuse, but rather there is not enough discussion about it.

The positive clearly does not offset the negative. This means even if there are one loving parent and one abusive parent, words are still damaging. Science backs up that repetitive verbal abuse can lead to the psychological impact such as having low self-esteem, anxeity, and can affect the development of brains. Many of those experienced verbal abuse in their childhood can still hear negative voices in their head, and it takes a long time to rid of this mental habit and realize that it is not the reality of the situation. It poisons the family relationship, in which is the most essential to children's fundamental development in life.
